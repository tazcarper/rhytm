Designing the Look

Design and polish how things look — just by asking

How to shape a new piece of the site — a section, a card, a pop-up — and fine-tune how it looks, all in plain language with Claude. You describe what you want; Claude builds it to match the rest of the site, you look at it in your browser, and you nudge it until it feels right.

1What this is for

This is the look-and-feel workflow: designing a new visual piece, or refining how an existing one looks. Wording, spacing, layout, a new pop-up, a nicer card — the things you can see.

2The part that makes this easy

The site has a written design system — its colours (the deep olive, the leather tan, the warm-paper background), its fonts, its spacing, the way buttons and cards look. Claude reads it before it designs anything, so new work matches the rest of the site automatically.

What that means for you: you don’t have to pick colours or fonts. Describe what you want in everyday words and it comes out looking like Rhythm Outdoors. If you ever want to be explicit, just say:

“Keep it on-brand — match the rest of the site.”

You can ask for a feel rather than specifics, too — “calmer,” “a bit more elegant,” “make it feel premium.” Claude knows what those mean for this brand.

3The workflow, step by step

1 · Describe what you want

Plain language. Say what it’s for and what should be in it — not how to build it.

“When someone’s picking add-ons during booking, I want a little pop-up that shows a photo of the add-on and a short description, so they understand what they’re getting before they choose it.”

2 · Let Claude build it

Claude designs and builds it using the site’s own look and its existing building blocks. You don’t need to do anything here except wait a moment.

3 · Look at it in your browser

You don’t ask Claude to “show” you — you look yourself. Claude keeps a private copy of the site running on your own computer at http://localhost:3000. Open it and refresh to see every change.

If the page won’t load, the site just isn’t running — say so and Claude will start it:

“The site isn’t loading — can you make sure it’s running so I can look at it in my browser?”

4 · Tweak it until it’s right

This is the fun part — say what you’d change, in plain words, and refresh.

“The photo’s too small — make it bigger and put it at the top.”

“There’s too much going on. Calm it down and give it more space.”

“On a phone, have it slide up from the bottom instead of floating in the middle.”

5 · When you’re happy

“This looks great. I’m done — open the pull request so my developer can review it.”

4Two ways to fine-tune the look

Step 4 above can happen two ways. Both are fine — pick whichever feels natural.

Just ask in chat

  • Type what you’d change; refresh your browser to see it.
  • Best for clear, specific changes (“smaller headline,” “more space”).
  • Nothing to set up — it’s the default.

Live mode (point & pick)

  • A visual mode: you point at a thing on the page and Claude shows a few versions side by side to choose from.
  • Little sliders let you fine-tune — tighter/looser spacing, more/less of a colour — without re-asking.
  • Best when you’re not sure what you want and want to see options. Ask: “Let’s use live mode to try a few versions of this.”

Either way, the changes land in the same place and ship the same way — through the pull request your developer reviews.

5Optional: sketch a look first

Most of the time, describing it to Claude and tweaking in your browser is the fastest path — start there. But if you’d like to see a few looks before anything is built — say, to show someone and agree on a direction — there’s a free web tool from Google called Stitch (stitch.withgoogle.com) that turns a description into a picture of a screen.

  • Ask Claude for a “Stitch prompt” for your idea — it’ll write the description for you, already matched to the brand.
  • Paste it into Stitch, in your web browser, and look at what it draws. Refine by chatting with it.
  • Bring the picture back to Claude as a reference: “build something like this, on-brand.”

Treat Stitch as a sketch, not the finished thing. It’s a separate website that lives in your browser only — it can’t change the real site, and its drawing isn’t the actual code. The real piece still gets built (and shipped) by Claude in the steps above. If you just want it built, you can skip Stitch entirely.

6Who does what

Your part

  • Describe what you want it to look like / do
  • Look at it on your own computer and react
  • Tweak the look with Claude until it feels right
  • Say “I’m done” so Claude opens the pull request

Your developer's part

  • Review the change
  • Apply anything that touches the live site or its data
  • Publish it

Look-only changes vs. ones with data. A pure look change (spacing, wording, a restyle) shows up correctly on the shared preview link right away. But if your new piece needs to store something — like a photo and description for each add-on — that part only shows on the shared link after your developer applies it. On your own computer (localhost:3000) you always see the real thing. Trust your local view while you work.
